Assessing VR for Emergency Response Rapid Review & Heuristic Evaluation

It is a research project following PRISMA-ScR guidelines. This review assesses the effectiveness of VR training for emergency responders, focusing on Situational Awareness, Emotional Engagement, and Cognitive Load. We analyzed studies from 2019-2025 to evaluate if VR can replace traditional life-saving training.

Team Members:
Walter Xu ( Researcher - Literature Screening, Data Synthesis, UX Analysis )
Hala Jaber ( Researcher - Literature Screening, Data Synthesis )
Ngoc Diem Quynh (Gwen) Vu ( Researcher - Literature Screening, Data Synthesis )

Duration: 3 Months

Research & Problem Statement

The motivation for this review stems from the high-stakes nature of emergency response. Traditional training is effective but dangerous and costly. VR offers a solution, yet usability issues often hinder learning. We identified a critical gap: How does VR user interface design affect a trainee's cognitive workload?

...

Methodology & Screening

Instead of market research, we conducted a Systematic Search across Laurier Library and Google Scholar. We filtered articles based on keywords like "Interaction Design" and "Firefighter," excluding AR and non-peer-reviewed papers. We found that high-fidelity simulations are double-edged swords: they improve immersion but can significantly increase cognitive overload if not designed with HFE (Human Factors) principles.

...

Key Findings - Interaction Cost

Analysis of the gathered studies revealed major usability flaws. For instance, 61% of experts experienced controller confusion in paediatric training. We found that standard gaming controllers violate the "Match between system and real world" heuristic. Trainees spent too much mental energy remembering buttons instead of fighting fires.

...

UX Recommendations

Based on the evidence, we synthesized specific design criteria for future VR systems:
Interactions: Replace abstract buttons with natural gestures (grabbing, pointing).
Guidance: Use diegetic UI cues (e.g., 3D arrows, wrist-timers) to reduce memory load.
Gradual Stress: Do not introduce environmental stressors (smoke, noise) until the user masters the controls.

...

Reflection

Through this rapid review, I learned that realism isn't everything. A highly realistic fire simulation fails if the UI is too complex. This project sharpened my ability to apply HFE (Human Factors and Ergonomics) theories to evaluate emerging technology. It bridged the gap between academic research and practical UX strategy.
